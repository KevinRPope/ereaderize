Connected

Posted by Susan Orlean

I've always been afraid of video games—not afraid that I wouldn't like them, but that I would like them too much, and that after mere seconds in front of any particularly bright and absorbing game, I would abandon all ambition, turn into a mouth-breathing zombie, and develop a wide, sofa-shaped rear end. As a result, I hadn't played a video game since Pac-Man. I knew nothing of Playstations and X-boxes and Wiis; I avoided them. When I heard about the Microsoft Kinect, though, I felt an urgency rising in me. A game you played without touching any machinery? A chance to wave your hands around, Minority-Report style, and move things around on a screen? This sounded like almost too much fun, with gadget-y pizzazz that sounded astonishing. Fear for my future aside, it was something I really wanted to try.

The Kinect is a long skinny piece of equipment with a little camera eye. It is connected to an Xbox, a computer game console that connects to a television monitor. You start the process of playing in a very interesting way—you are instructed to move furniture out of your way, which is not something that ever was required in order to play Pac-Man. Then you wave at the Kinect, and it peers at you like a little robot, sizing you up and putting you into the game on the screen. You aren't an avatar, mix-and-matched with a cartoon person; this is you as you, beamed into the actual game. I have no idea how this happens, but it does, which is in itself pretty magical. And then you play. I realize now that the Kinect isn't a video game; it's an activity environment that happens to be on a screen. Someone who didn't play hooky during science class as often as I did could probably explain this better. The closest thing I can compare it to is making shadow puppets with your hands on a wall; your hand is still your hand but it's also the shadow creature, and you manipulate them both, simultaneously.

I heard a computer scientist the other day refer to playing with the Kinect as “storytelling.” At first I thought that sounded a little high-minded, but after trying a few games I could see what she meant. There is a sort of “story” happening on the screen, and you're part of it, directing it, advancing it by your movements and actions. It's very absorbing, and even watching someone else play is fascinating. Maybe in the future this technology will become so commonplace that it won't seem so amazing, but right now, it's hard to tear yourself away. There is an animal game for kids that my son played the other day. It begins with the player “adopting” a lion or tiger cub, and before you start the competitive part of the game, you have a chance to pet your cub. Of course, this means you are stroking the air in front of you, but the cub onscreen responds, purring gratefully as you pet its tickle spots. I could have watched my son petting his tiger forever; it was a rare blend of a crazy sci-fi future-now experience and something very primal and sweet.

Whoever designed this mind-boggling device must have a good sense of humor. On Friday, I tried out Dance Central, a program that teaches you dance moves and then has a dance-off to prove your mastery. I decided to start with Lady Gaga's “Poker Face,” and after I waved at my television screen to show that I was ready, the song began, and I met my teacher. To be honest, I was expecting the instructor avatar to be blonde and chirpy, a Lesley College dance major type, and the setting to be a brightly lit studio. Instead, the “scene” I entered was a ratty looking club filled with vaguely menacing patrons, and my instructor was an African-American teenager wearing a sweatshirt with a hood pulled all the way over his eyes and nose. Take that, you living-room gangsters! Mr. Hood never cracked a smile, but he was a great teacher, and by the end of a sweaty hour we were fist pumping in unison, he on screen and me in my living room, and I ended up with a spectacular dance-off score.
